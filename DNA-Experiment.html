DNA Extraction and Preparation

Before beginning our experiment, we reviewed the instruction guide on Google Classroom to prepare for collecting our cheek cells. We gently scraped the inside of our cheeks three to four times with a flat-end toothpick, making sure not to eat or chew gum beforehand for the best results. We then dipped the toothpick into the X-Tract™ buffer, swirling it thoroughly to dislodge and suspend the cells. This specialized extraction buffer works to lyse, or break open, the cellular and nuclear membranes, releasing the genomic DNA directly into the solution. By heating this mixture at 95 degrees Celsius for 10 minutes, the buffer stabilizes the liberated DNA while safely inactivating any cellular enzymes that could degrade our sample, leaving us with a raw DNA template ready for amplification

PCR Setup and Amplification

With our genomic DNA isolated, we began setting up the PCR by labeling a fresh 200-microliter tube. Using a micropipette with fresh tips to prevent contamination, we added 12 microliters of Master Mix and 12 microliters of D1S80 Primer Mix. We then introduced 2 microliters of our newly extracted student DNA template into the mixture. Next, we secured the tube inside the miniPCR thermal cycler and closed the lid tightly. Finally, we programmed the software to run 32 cycles of denaturation, annealing, and extension to copy our target sequence millions of times.

Gel Casting and Solidification

To prepare the gel, dissolve one Agarose Tab in 30 mL of TBE buffer inside a flask, letting it sit for a few minutes before swirling into a uniform suspension. Heating the mixture for roughly 60 seconds until it reaches a clear, rolling boil, monitoring closely to prevent boil-over. Finally, using a heatproof mitt, slowly pour the molten agarose into the assembled mold and let it sit undisturbed for 10 minutes to solidify

Gel Setup and Buffer Addition

After the gel solidified, we removed the comb and supports, revealing the wells formed by the comb’s teeth . We reinserted the Electrodams™ upside down so the electrode wires faced the gel, placing the black Electrodam™ near the wells and the red one at the far end . Then we added 30 mL of 1X TBE buffer to cover the gel and fill the wells, since the buffer conducts electricity through the system .

Sample Loading and Circuit Activation

Using a micropipette, we loaded our PCR samples by placing the tip just inside each well and slowly dispensing to the first stop to avoid bubbles . After connecting the black and red alligator clips to their matching Electrodams™, we powered the Bandit™ controller and checked for bubbles at the electrodes to confirm current flow .

DNA Separation by Electrophoresis

During electrophoresis, the negatively charged DNA fragments were pushed away from the negative electrode near the wells and pulled toward the positive electrode. Smaller fragments moved faster through the agarose “web,” while larger fragments lagged behind, creating separated bands based on size .

Digital Analysis and Fragment Size Calculation

After the run finished, we downloaded a clear photo of our gel and opened it in Photoshop. Using the ruler tool, we measured the ladder bands, our sample, and a few other student samples in pixels. We entered all pixel distances into our individual Google Sheet, along with the slope and intercept from the line‑of‑best‑fit graph. The spreadsheet formulas then calculated the fragment sizes for our D1S80 alleles to compare them to our classmates.

Is Race socially and psychologically real?

Race is real socially and psychologically. One example of some evidence that proves that race is psychologically real is the implicit association test open parentheses IAT). The test first asked participants to sort images of people who are “white” and people who are “black”. then it asked them to sort positive sounding words and negative sounding words. Finally the test switches which side the faces or words belong on and because the test moves quickly people will rely less on conscious thought and more automatic associations that show all of the Hidden biases in identifying race. In the end, the results showed that about 70% of the test takers pair the positive words with white people and negative words with black people. This shows that race is psychologically real and socially real because this test relied on less conscious thought and more on unconscious associations. Another example is Jane Elliott's famous classroom experiment. She told her third grade students that brown-eyed children were inferior to the blue-eyed children and she also made the brown-eyed children wear collared shirts to separate them. Then, she reversed the rules and labeled the blue-eyed students as inferior and treated them the same way that the brown-eyed students were treated the day before. When her experiment concluded, many of their students said that it was harder to think and learn underneath the discrimination.for something to be socially or psychologically real, it must have measurable effects on people's lives. I think that race fits this criteria because tests like the IAT and Elliott's experiment show that race can have a harmful performance on mental well-being and discrimination. The IAT even shows consistent racial bias with most participants unconsciously deciding that black people were more negative.

This enhanced bias because most people felt safer around people like themselves.
